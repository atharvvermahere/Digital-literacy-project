Case Study: The Midnight Encryption (Ransomware)
What is the Crime?
Ransomware is a type of malware designed to deny a user or organization access to their own files by encrypting them. The attackers then demand a ransom payment—usually in cryptocurrency like Bitcoin—in exchange for the decryption key, often threatening to delete or leak the data if not paid.

How it Typically Happens (Step-by-Step):
Infiltration: An employee receives a "spear-phishing" email that looks like a legitimate invoice. They click a link or download a malicious attachment (e.g., a .zip or .doc file).
Deployment: Once opened, the malware silently executes in the background. It communicates with the attacker’s "Command and Control" server to generate an encryption key.
Encryption: The software begins locking files, focusing on databases, documents, and even cloud backups connected to the local network.
The Demand: A "Ransom Note" appears as a desktop wallpaper or a text file in every folder, providing instructions on how to pay for the recovery.

Who is Typically Targeted?
While individuals are at risk, attackers primarily target hospitals, schools, and government agencies. These organizations are chosen because they provide critical services and cannot afford long periods of downtime, making them more likely to pay quickly.

The Consequences:
The fallout is devastating. Beyond the financial loss of the ransom (which can reach millions), victims face massive operational shutdowns, permanent loss of sensitive data if the key doesn't work, and significant reputational damage. Legal costs and cybersecurity upgrades following the attack often exceed the cost of the ransom itself.
